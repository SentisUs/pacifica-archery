## 1. Executive summary

Pacifica Archery is a genuinely strong business with a **discovery problem, a scheduling problem, and a yield problem** — in that order of severity. The original report nailed the first two. This analysis confirms them with hard data and adds the third.

- **Demand is real and structurally supported.** ~19 million Americans shoot target archery, the majority recreational rather than hunting [FWS 2022]. Pacifica sits in a ~1.5M-person, affluent, **57.5%-Asian** Daly City catchment [US Census via Wikipedia] where archery carries deep cultural resonance (Korea has won the women's Olympic team gold **10 straight times, 1988–2024** [Olympics.com]). Daly City is the "Pinoy Capital" with the highest Filipino concentration of any mid-sized North American city.
- **The moat is real.** Pacifica is the only full-service indoor range in the SF–northern-Peninsula corridor — weather-proof, year-round, with in-house fitting, tuning, leagues, JOAD, and free freeway-adjacent parking. The nearest competitor (SF Archery Shop) is a rental counter feeding the outdoor Golden Gate Park range, publishes **no prices**, and has **no online booking**.
- **The problem is conversion, not interest.** Prices live on an image-only fee sheet that search engines and phone visitors never read; the social presence is under-used; and the shop is **closed Sunday and Monday** — Sunday being the single highest-intent leisure day and the exact day the competitor is open.
- **The new finding — yield.** Pacifica is **not under-priced**; its $22 lane + $17 rental ($39 all-in/person/hour) already sits at the *top* of Bay Area experiential willingness-to-pay (climbing $30–35, axe-throwing $20–37, VR ~$39). The opportunity is therefore **not to raise the base price but to vary it by day** — discounting the dead midweek hours to fill idle lanes while holding the weekend at today's published rate. This is textbook revenue management for a perishable, fixed-capacity asset.

**Bottom line:** fix the free items (Google Business Profile, publish prices as text, short-form video, the new website), reallocate hours to open Sunday **without adding labor cost**, and roll out a **weekday-value / weekend-regular pricing structure** to convert latent demand into booked, higher-utilization lane-hours. None of this requires meaningful capital.

## 6. The flexible pricing model (math + psychology + sales)

### 6.1 The core idea
An archery lane-hour is **perishable, fixed-capacity inventory** — the same economics as a hotel room, an airline seat, or a golf tee-time. It passes all three revenue-management tests: (a) fixed capacity (15 lanes), (b) perishable inventory (an empty Tuesday-2pm lane is lost forever), and (c) time-variable demand (weekends peak) [Cornell/Kimes, RevPAR]. The correct objective is to **maximize revenue per available lane-hour (RevPALH) = utilization × average price**, not headcount or price alone.

The model has **one governing principle that resolves every design choice and every fairness concern:**

> **The weekend is the regular, published price. Weekdays are a labeled discount off it. No price ever goes *up* from today's fee sheet.**

This is not a cosmetic framing choice — it is the single most important decision in the redesign, and it is grounded in the fairness research below.

### 6.2 Why discount-down, never premium-up (the fairness math)
- **Loss aversion:** losses loom ~2.25× larger than equivalent gains [Kahneman/Tversky]. Framing the weekday as "save $X" is a *gain*; framing the weekend as "+$X" is a *loss* felt twice as hard for identical dollars.
- **Dual-entitlement:** a price *increase* that exploits demand is judged unfair (82% called a snow-day shovel hike unfair), but a *discount* off a stable reference is judged fair [Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler 1986].
- **The Wendy's lesson:** merely *naming* "dynamic/surge pricing" triggered #BoycottWendys in 2024 even with no increase planned; they recovered only by reframing it as **discounts during slower times** [CNN]. Uber surge is resented because it's opaque and volatile [Rice/Kinder].
- **Therefore:** customer-facing copy uses **"Weekday Savings — save up to 30%"** and named windows ("Midweek Hunter Hours"), and **never** the words *surge, dynamic, premium, or peak.* The weekend price equals today's published price, so no existing customer ever pays more than they do now.

### 6.3 The discount magnitude (why ~25%, and ~40% for the dead window)
- A price change must clear the **just-noticeable-difference** (~8–12% generally, ~15–20% for considered/experiential purchases) or it's ignored [Weber's Law; JND literature]. A $2-off-$22 (9%) weekday rate would barely register.
- Proven off-peak leisure discounts that actually shift traffic sit at **~25% (matinees) to ~25–50% (Hunter Hours)** and *expand* total volume rather than merely shifting it — cinema demand is highly elastic (own-price elasticity ≈ −1.7 to −3.9) and "Cheap Tuesday" creates net-new trips [de Roos & McKenzie 2014; AMC matinee $10.39 vs $13.99].
- Comparable Bay Area / leisure differentials: weeknight bowling +12–40%, escape rooms +10–25%, climbing weekday-matinee ~14–17% off. The lone archery precedent — **Easton Salt Lake — runs a literal 2× peak ($6 off-peak vs $12 peak)**, but off a very low base.
- **Design choice:** **~25% weekday discount** off the weekend anchor (above JND, within fairness norms, matched to proven magnitudes), and a deeper **~40% "Midweek Hunter Hours"** on the genuinely dead Wed–Fri pre-5pm window, where any booking above variable cost beats an empty lane.

### 6.5 Psychology baked into the menu (sales coherence)
- **Good-Better-Best, target in the middle.** The homepage front options show three choices — **Just the Range** (components, "good"), the **⭐ 2 Archers Bundle $85** ("better", center, flagged Most Popular), and **Group Events** ("best"/anchor). The **2 Archers Bundle sits in the center** — the compromise effect + center-stage effect both push share to the middle option, and the effect is *stronger when buying for others* (i.e. dates/groups), which is most archery outings [Simonson & Tversky; Valenzuela & Raghubir; HBR GBB].
- **Anchoring + decoy.** Bundles show the **strikethrough "normally" price** ($95, $158) so the discounted bundle reads as a clear win; the high Private Group ($1,800) and Advanced Training figures anchor the menu so everything else looks reasonable by comparison [Ariely; Economist decoy study].
- **Round vs charm numbers.** Experiential/premium prices use **round numbers** ($22, $40, $130) which feel trustworthy and fluent; the **weekday "value" rate is where a charm/edge number signals a bargain** ($59, $99, $179) [Wadhwa & Zhang 2015; Anderson & Simester $9 study].
- **Bundle to homogenize valuations.** Couples/2/4-archer packages sell a single combined price with gear & lanes "included" rather than per-person × N — bundling profits the seller precisely when group valuations are more uniform than the parts [Adams & Yellen].

### 6.6 Illustrative yield model (directional — replace with real utilization)
*The shop should drop its actual booking counts in; the structure is what matters.* New schedule (§7) = 15 lanes × 41 open hours = **615 available lane-hours/week**.

Suppose today's flat $22 lane yields these rough utilizations and the new structure shifts demand off the peak and fills the dead window:

| Band | Lane-hrs/wk (avail.) | *Illustrative* utilization → | Lane price (flat → tiered) | Lane revenue/wk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hunter Hours (Wed–Fri <5pm) | ~180 | 15% → **35%** | $22 → **$15** | $594 → **$945** |
| Weekday eve (Wed–Fri 5–8) | ~135 | 35% → **45%** | $22 → **$20** | $1,040 → **$1,215** |
| Weekend (Sat & Sun 10–8) | ~300 | 70% → **80%** | $22 → **$25** | $4,620 → **$6,000** |
| **Total lane rev/wk** | 615 | | | **$6,254 → $8,160 (+30.5%)** |

The gain comes from **filling idle midweek capacity** (a discounted booking beats an empty lane), from **adding Sunday**, and from a modest round-up of the weekend rate. The bulk is demand-shifting, not a price hike — Happy-Hour and weekday rates are genuine discounts. Equipment rental and bundle attach-rates layer on top — and rentals are the highest-margin add-on, so driving the **Range Pass** lifts blended margin further. **These percentages are placeholders;** the model's job is to show that even modest demand-shifting is RevPALH-positive when the alternative is an empty lane earning $0.

### 6.7 Guardrails
- Hold a small block of weekend prime-time lanes for full-price walk-ins (don't let cheap advance weekday bookings cannibalize the constrained peak).
- Keep the structure **stable and predictable** (named windows, posted) — never real-time/opaque.
- If any rate ever rises, tie it to a **cost story** (new bows, certified coaches), never "because it's busy."
- Track **"how did you hear about us?"** and band-level utilization at the desk for 60 days, then tune.

## 7. Full-week schedule — redistribute hours onto the weekend (adopted from §5)

The schedule below **redistributes hours off dead weekday mornings and onto the weekend**: it closes Monday *and* Tuesday, opens a full Sunday, extends Saturday to 8pm, and runs Wed–Fri 1–8. Total staffed hours actually **fall by 4 per week** (~200 hours/year), and the savings come entirely out of low-traffic weekday mornings — not the weekend.

| Day | Today | **Proposed** | Hours | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Monday | Closed | Closed | — | Genuinely low demand; stay closed. |
| Tuesday | 11–8 (9h) | **Closed** | — | Low demand across the day; close it. |
| Wednesday | 11–8 (9h) | **1:00–8:00 PM** | 7 | Trim the dead late-morning; keep the active 5–8 league/lessons window. |
| Thursday | 11–8 (9h) | **1:00–8:00 PM** | 7 | Same (Thu 4:30 youth class preserved). |
| Friday | 11–8 (9h) | **1:00–8:00 PM** | 7 | Focus on the after-work / date-night window. |
| Saturday | 10–7 (9h) | **10:00 AM–8:00 PM** | 10 | Peak day — extended an hour later. |
| Sunday | Closed | **10:00 AM–8:00 PM** | 10 | Co-peak; the highest day the shop currently forfeits to the competitor. |
| **Total** | **45h** | **41h** | **−4** | **Fewer staffed hours; Sunday recovered; only dead weekday mornings removed.** |

This is the highest-impact operational change available and it **lowers labor cost** while opening on the highest-demand day the shop currently forfeits. Confirm the new opens/closes against the actual Saturday traffic curve and staff availability, then update Google, the website, and the door signage everywhere at once.

**Program the peaks:** beginner/date/group promos aimed at Saturday, Sunday, and Friday evening (new customers); leagues & JOAD anchored on weekday evenings, Wed–Fri 5–8 (existing regulars). ## 10. Risks

- **Execution bandwidth.** A small family business can't do all eight at once. Sequence: free items first (GBP, site, prices, video), then Sunday, then pricing rollout. The 90-day plan (§12) sequences this.
- **Fairness perception of tiered pricing.** Fully mitigated by the discount-down framing (§6.2). Do **not** deviate into surge/premium language.
- **Cannibalization.** Aggressive weekday discounts could pull would-be weekend customers midweek. Mitigated by the weekend-prime guardrail and by the fact that weekend demand is time-locked (people who can only come Saturday will still come Saturday).
- **The competitor wakes up.** SF Archery launching a real site/booking erodes the digital head start. This is a reason to move *now*, not later.
- **Over-reliance on the Hunger Games effect.** The 2026 film is a *plan-around* catalyst, not a guarantee — the documented surge is from 2012–2015 only. Don't budget on it; treat upside as a bonus.
- **"A little run down" reviews.** A cheap refresh (lighting, declutter, paint, photo backdrops) directly improves both the in-person experience and the video/photo content. Low cost, compounding benefit.
- **Booking/waiver continuity.** The new site routes "Book Now" to the existing booking + waiver system via a single swap-in URL (§11) — verify the live link before publishing so no booking is lost.
